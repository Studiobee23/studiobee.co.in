IA + Navigation Design05 / 21
IA + Navigation Design
Single-page scroll with purposeful hierarchy.
Every section order was a design decision. The user arrives at the Hero, sees proof of work immediately (Work section is #2), then understands how it's made (Services, Process), meets the people (Team), and reaches the call to action.
Site Architecture
Navigation Design Rationale
Why Fixed Nav?
Persistent access, persistent CTA
Fixed position keeps the CTA ('Start a project') always visible. As the user scrolls, the nav gains a blur/opacity background — signalling context change without a full redesign.
Why a Button CTA in Nav?
Visual grammar: text links navigate, buttons act
'Start a project' is styled as a pill button, not a text link. It creates a clear visual hierarchy: text links (navigate) vs button (act). The only nav item that's a conversion action gets the only button treatment.
Primary path

Hero & Navigation — Wireframes06 / 21
Section Design Process
The hero was the hardest page to get right.
Three hero layouts were explored. The final design takes Concept A's strong typographic rhythm and Concept B's left-alignment (proven in eye-tracking to be read first), then adds the wave strip system — a fourth element unique to studiobee that no concept had, born from the brand's kinetic energy.
Concept A — Centered layout, static bg
Full-center symmetry, all elements axis-aligned. Clean hierarchy but lacks directional energy.
Concept B — Left text, right geometric
Left-aligned copy with abstract geometric shapes on the right. Natural reading flow, but geometric shapes feel generic.
Concept C — Full-bleed image overlay
Image fills the entire hero, text anchored bottom-left with dark overlay. Dramatic, but image-dependent and less brand-controlled.
Final Direction — Left-aligned + wave strips
B's left-alignment + A's type hierarchy + a brand-unique wave strip system that emerged from the identity itself.
What each concept offered
A offered clean typographic hierarchy with strong visual rhythm. B proved left-alignment creates a more natural reading flow — eye-tracking validates left-anchored layouts. C showed that full-bleed backgrounds create drama and immersion, though image-dependency is a risk.
Why this combination
B's left alignment and A's type hierarchy form the structural base. The wave strip system emerged not from any concept, but from the brand identity itself — it made the hero unlike any agency site we benchmarked. It is the brand made visible.
Services Section — Wireframes07 / 21
Section Design Process
Six services, zero feature-list feeling.
Three layout systems were explored. The final design combines C's clean 3-col grid with B's visual weight variation — one card spans 2 columns, giving hierarchy to the primary service without breaking the grid logic.
Concept A — 2-col list with icons
Two columns, icon + title + description per row. Linear and readable but feels like a feature list, not a service offering.
Concept B — Masonry, unequal heights
Cards at varying heights create visual variety, but hierarchy is unclear — which service is primary? Scanning is harder.
Concept C — 3-col equal grid
Clean 3×2 grid, all cards equal. Great structure but flat hierarchy — the primary service disappears into the grid.
Final Direction — 3-col with span-2 card
Primary service spans 2 of 3 columns in row 1, establishing hierarchy. Rows 2–3 revert to equal 3-col cards. Hierarchy without breaking the grid.
What each concept offered
A's 2-col list was scannable but felt like a spec sheet. B's masonry gave visual energy but sacrificed clarity. C's 3-col grid had the cleanest structure — it just treated all services as equal, which they aren't.
Why this combination
C's grid is the foundation. B's insight — that one element can carry more visual weight — is applied by making the primary service span 2 columns in blue. The client's headline service earns its prominence. The grid logic stays intact.
Work Carousel — Wireframes08 / 21
Section Design Process
The most important conversion point on the page.
Three display approaches were explored. The final design takes B's horizontal scroll behaviour and C's tall 9:16 portrait format — creating a marquee that feels like scrolling through an Instagram-native content feed, which matches how the target audience consumes visual content.
Concept A — Static thumbnail grid
3×3 equal square thumbnails in a gallery layout. Comprehensive but static — no invitation to explore, no sense of 'more'.
Concept B — Horizontal scroll with peek
4 cards in a row with the last partially cropped, inviting horizontal scroll. Arrow controls feel desktop-centric.
Concept C — Full-bleed poster, one at a time
One portrait card fills the viewport, dot navigation below. Strong visual impact, but lacks the sense of volume — doesn't show 'we have lots of work'.
Final Direction — Infinite marquee, 9:16 cards
Tall portrait cards auto-scroll in an infinite marquee. Mobile-native ratio, sense of volume, hover pauses — respects user intent.
What each concept offered
A's grid showed all work at once but felt static. B's horizontal scroll gave momentum and suggested depth. C's tall portrait format felt native and premium — like premium Instagram content.
Why this combination
B's horizontal scroll format combined with C's 9:16 ratio creates a marquee that feels native to how the target audience consumes content on their phones. Auto-scroll removes friction; hover-pause respects intent. No arrows needed.
Stats Section — Wireframes09 / 21
Section Design Process
Credibility communicated in under 2 seconds.
Stats needed to communicate credibility at a glance. Three approaches were tested. The final design combines A's row layout with C's coloured accent bars — large numbers with short blue bars above them create visual anchors that guide the eye without adding complexity.
Concept A — Plain numbers in a row
4 large numbers left-aligned with labels below. Clean but visually flat — the numbers lack entry points for the eye to land on.
Concept B — Large type with stacked descriptors
Hero-scale numbers with extended description text below each. Dramatic but the long descriptors compete with the numbers for attention.
Concept C — Numbers with accent bars
Coloured horizontal bars sit above each number, creating rhythmic visual anchors. The bars guide the eye to the numbers below.
Final Direction — Large numbers + short accent bars
4-col layout: blue accent bar above each number creates an entry point, large type delivers the stat, short label completes it. Scannable in under 2 seconds.
What each concept offered
A proved large type is the right scale — numbers should dominate. B showed that extended descriptors reduce legibility for a stats section. C's bars gave the insight: visual entry points guide the eye to the numbers that matter.
Why this combination
A's large-type approach with C's bar system. The short blue accent bar above each number acts as a visual landing strip — the eye arrives at the bar, drops to the number, then reads the label. Intentional reading order designed into the layout.
Process Section — Wireframes10 / 21
Section Design Process
Four steps — not a checklist, a story.
The process section needed to show 4 steps clearly without feeling like a linear checklist. Three approaches were explored. The final design takes A's numbered system and B's horizontal format, then adds a micro-interaction from C's expand concept — a blue reveal bar animates up from the bottom on hover.
Concept A — Numbered vertical list
4 stacked rows with large number prefix, title, and description. Clear but reads as a document, not a designed section.
Concept B — Horizontal timeline
4 circle nodes on a horizontal line with titles above and descriptions below. Communicates flow well but feels more like an infographic than a designed section.
Concept C — Accordion
4 rows, only the active step is expanded. Reduces visual clutter but hides content — requires interaction to see all steps simultaneously.
Final Direction — 4-col grid + hover bar
4 equal columns with large step number, title, description. A blue bar animates up from the card bottom on hover — a reveal mechanic inspired by C's accordion.
What each concept offered
A's numbered system gave clarity and a natural reading order. B's horizontal format suggested progression and made the steps feel parallel, not sequential. C's accordion introduced the idea of information revealed through interaction — a micro-interaction principle worth keeping.
Why this combination
B's 4-col horizontal grid with A's numbering creates instant structure. C's reveal mechanic becomes the hover animation — a blue bar that rises from the bottom of each card on hover. All content is visible at once (unlike C), but interaction still rewards engagement.
Testimonials — Wireframes11 / 21
Section Design Process
Social proof that feels tactile, not templated.
Three approaches to displaying client testimonials were explored. The final design uses C's stacked deck — the only concept that felt tactile and differentiated. The drag-to-advance mechanic came from rethinking A's swipe gesture in the context of C's physical card metaphor.
Concept A — Carousel with dots/arrows
Standard carousel with left/right arrows and dot indicators. Familiar but expected — no differentiation from any other agency site.
Concept B — 2×2 grid of cards
Four quote cards displayed simultaneously in a 2×2 grid. Comprehensive but flat — no card stands out, and the section feels like a data table.
Concept C — Stacked rotated deck
3 cards in a stacked deck at slight rotations. The layering creates depth and physicality — unlike any other testimonial pattern we benchmarked.
Final Direction — Rotated stack + drag gesture
C's deck visual with the front card slightly lifted + a drag affordance. The physical card metaphor makes the swipe gesture feel natural.
What each concept offered
A was familiar but expected — any competitor would do the same. B showed all quotes simultaneously but removed the sense of 'more to discover'. C's stacked deck was immediately differentiated — tactile, physical, and brand-appropriate.
Why this combination
C's deck is the core. A's swipe gesture is rethought as a drag mechanic — instead of arrows, you physically drag the front card left to advance. The card metaphor makes the gesture feel logical. The rotation angles reinforce the physicality: this is a stack of real endorsements.
Team Section — Wireframes12 / 21
Section Design Process
Warm, human — and earned through interaction.
The team section sat on a warm cream background. Three concepts were explored for the individual team member cards. The final design combines B's greyscale-to-colour effect with C's information reveal panel — creating a card that rewards curiosity without front-loading text.
Concept A — Grid + text overlay on hover
Name and role overlay appear on hover. Simple, familiar — but hides the person behind the interaction requirement and feels predictable.
Concept B — Greyscale photos, colour on hover
All team photos default to greyscale, revealing full colour on hover. Creates visual discipline and makes the section feel considered, not chaotic.
Concept C — Split: photo + bio panel
Each card has photo on the left and a bio panel on the right. Comprehensive but front-loads information — the bio competes with the photo before engagement happens.
Final Direction — Greyscale + blue reveal panel
Greyscale default (from B) + a blue info panel that slides up from the bottom on hover (from C). Colour reveals simultaneously with bio — the reward is double.
What each concept offered
A's overlay was familiar but expected. B's greyscale insight was the strongest visual idea — it makes the layout work on structure alone and turns colour into a reward. C's split panel showed that bio information should be contextualised, not front-loaded.
Why this combination
B's greyscale discipline is the base — it creates a unified, calm grid that lets the cream background breathe. C's panel is reimagined: instead of always-visible, it slides up on hover in blue — matching the brand colour and making the entire reveal moment (colour + bio) happen simultaneously as a single interaction reward.
CTA / Contact Form — Wireframes13 / 21
Section Design Process
The closing argument deserves a designed moment.
The contact section is the final conversion point. Three form presentations were explored. The final design uses C's minimal centered approach on a solid blue background, then adds A's animated success state — making the completion moment feel like a celebration, not just a system message.
Concept A — Full-screen bg form
Dark hero-style background with a semi-transparent form overlay. Dramatic but the decorative bg reduces focus on the actual form fields.
Concept B — Split: left visual + right form
Left half is a branded decorative panel, right half contains the form. The split creates two competing focal points — the form loses the final section's urgency.
Concept C — Centered minimal on colour
Form centered on a solid blue background, no visual decoration competing. Undivided focus on the conversion action — and the blue makes it feel like an arrival, not just a form.
Final Direction — Centered blue + animated success
C's centered minimal form on blue + A's dramatic success moment: animated SVG checkmark + countdown bar. Completion is a designed reward.
What each concept offered
A's dramatic style brought the insight that the success state should feel momentous — the completion of a journey deserves a celebration. B showed that splitting attention at the final CTA loses the sense of a single, important action. C proved that reducing visual competition maximises focus on the form.
Why this combination
C is the foundation: undistracted focus, blue section that signals 'this is the destination'. A contributes the success state — an animated SVG checkmark with a circle stroke animation, followed by a 5-second countdown bar. Submitting the form becomes a satisfying, human moment.
Mobile Responsive — Wireframes14 / 21
Section Design Process
The nav had to work on a 390px screen without compromise.
Mobile navigation required a distinct design approach for small screens. Three concepts were explored. The final hamburger design combines B's clean trigger icon with C's full-screen overlay — creating an immersive menu that matches the site's premium character.
Concept A — Condensed sticky nav
All nav items remain visible in a compressed bar. On a 390px screen, items become too small to tap reliably — touch targets fail WCAG 2.5.5 minimum 44px.
Concept B — Hamburger + slide-in drawer
Hamburger trigger, sliding right-side drawer with links and a dimmed content behind. Clean trigger but the narrow drawer feels cramped — the type can't breathe.
Concept C — Hamburger + full-screen overlay
Hamburger triggers a full dark-screen menu with large display-size link text. Every touch target is enormous — and the scale feels like a brand decision, not a mobile compromise.
Final Direction — Hamburger + full-screen dark menu
B's minimal hamburger trigger + C's full-screen dark overlay. Large type links feel intentional — this is a menu designed for the space it has, not crammed into a drawer.
What each concept offered
A failed immediately on mobile — too small to tap. B's hamburger trigger was the right start, but the narrow drawer compromised the type scale. C's full-screen approach was the breakthrough: at full screen, the menu becomes a moment in itself, matching the site's premium positioning.
Why this combination
B provides the clean, minimal hamburger icon in the nav bar (three lines, no label). C provides the open state: a full dark screen with large DM Sans links at a size that signals intent. The result is a mobile menu that doesn't apologise for being mobile — it uses the full screen as a canvas.
Designing for Constraints15 / 21
Design for Constraints
When limitations made the design better.
Four moments during the project where a technical or device constraint forced a design rethink — and in each case, the constraint produced a more considered result.
Animation Audit
The hero had 50 animated elements competing for attention — and was crashing iPhones. We treated animation like typography: only what communicates energy earns its place. We reduced, organised, and only kept motion that reinforced the brand's kinetic character.
Design Decision
A leaner animation system that actually says more. The wave strips were kept because they ARE the hero — the rest was noise. Every removed animation made the remaining ones more powerful.
Ghost Button Duplication
The ghost button appeared to duplicate when it moved through the wave strip inversion system. Animating the whole button broke the illusion — two versions of the button appeared to exist simultaneously.
Design Decision
We redesigned the interaction: only the arrow icon inside the button moves on hover. This made the interaction feel more intentional — a directed gesture, not a floating shape. The constraint forced a more precise micro-interaction.
Texture Rendering Artefacts
A CSS filter for the grain texture caused the entire page to render on one compositor layer, introducing flicker and colour artefacts on mobile — especially on Android Chrome.
Design Decision
Rebuilt the grain as an inline SVG noise pattern using feTurbulence. Same tactile depth, fully design-controlled, renders without artefacts. We gained more granular control over the effect in the process — frequency, intensity, blending.
Blurred Badge Components
Blurred glass badges on 30+ work cards created a heavy GPU load on lower-performance devices — and visually looked cluttered, blurring both the badge content and the image beneath it.
Design Decision
Replaced backdrop-filter blur with a solid rgba() overlay. The result was actually cleaner and more legible — a reminder that constraints often push toward simplicity. The card labels became sharper, faster, and better.

Spring Easing

Case Studies Page Design18 / 21
Case Studies Page Design
Nine projects, one page, no uniform grid.
The case-studies.html page was designed separately from the main site. The challenge: display 9 projects in a way that felt curated, not templated. A uniform grid would have looked like a stock template — every project treated with identical weight, which no curated portfolio should do.
Four Grid Approaches Considered
A — Uniform 3×3
All same size — feels like a template, not a portfolio
B — Full masonry
Organic feel but no system — random, not curated
C — Mixed portrait+landscape
Some tall, some wide, no system — hard to scan
D — Bento asymmetric (Final)
Each card has a role — portrait, wide, or square
Final Bento Layout Rationale
Card Roles
Every card has a job, not just a slot
Portrait cards (9:16) for hero case studies — scrollable like Instagram, dominant in the grid. Wide cards (2-col span) for work with strong horizontal compositions. Square cards for logo/brand work that needs equal framing on both axes.
Interaction Decisions
Hover on desktop, tap-to-reveal on mobile
Hover: card scales 1.02x + colour overlay fades in. No hover text overlay on touch — tapping once reveals the project info panel, tapping again navigates to the case study. Keeps the grid clean on desktop, functional on mobile without hover states.

Micro-Interactions20 / 21
Micro-Interactions
The details that signal intent.
Hero Headings
Text Scramble on viewport entry
Characters randomise through the alphabet before resolving to the final word — a digital/generative brand signal that plays only on first impression. Uses IntersectionObserver to trigger once per page load.
Chose scramble over fade-in because it's active, not passive — the heading earns your attention by performing before delivering.
Navigation
Letters invert across wave boundaries
As wave strips pass across the nav, the logo and nav links visually 'invert' colour — dark letters become light, light become dark — using clip-path inversion strips positioned identically to the hero wave system.
Not planned — discovered during build. Kept because it makes the nav feel like part of the animation, not sitting on top of it. The nav participates in the brand.
Team Section
Greyscale default, colour on hover
All team photos default to greyscale via CSS filter. Hovering removes the filter, revealing full colour simultaneously with a blue bio panel sliding up from the card bottom. Colour is the reward for engagement.
Greyscale forces the layout to work on structure alone — the grid has to be good before colour makes it great. It's a discipline check built into the design.
Testimonials
Physical drag gesture on card stack
Instead of dots or arrows, the front testimonial card can be dragged left to advance. The rotated deck behind it amplifies the physical card metaphor — you're moving through a stack of endorsements, not paginating through data.
Drag feels more human than clicking an arrow. It maps the metaphor of physically sorting through cards to the digital interaction — the gesture IS the metaphor.
Contact Form
Animated SVG checkmark + progress bar
On submission: form fades out, an SVG checkmark animates in with a circle stroke-dashoffset animation, followed by a 5-second countdown bar before the form resets. The human moment is designed, not left to the browser default.
The success state is a designed moment, not a system message. It communicates: we received it, we're human, here's proof. The countdown bar sets expectation about what happens next.
Work Carousel
Auto-scroll pauses on hover intent
The infinite marquee pauses its requestAnimationFrame loop when the cursor enters the carousel track. It resumes on mouseleave. An IntersectionObserver also cancels the RAF entirely when the section scrolls off-screen, saving battery on mobile.
Auto-scroll that ignores hover intent feels broken and disrespectful. Pause on hover is the difference between a feature that works and a feature that respects the user.
